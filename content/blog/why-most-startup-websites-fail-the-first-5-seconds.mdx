A prospect visits your homepage. They give you about five seconds before they decide whether to keep reading or close the tab. That is not a marketing cliche — it is what we see in every session recording, on every site we audit, across every industry.

In those five seconds, they are running six checks in parallel. Most startup sites quietly fail at least four of them. Here is the list, in the order a real prospect runs it.

## 1. Does this load before I lose interest?

A 4-second hero is a dead hero. Mobile prospects on a mid-tier phone with mid-tier signal — which is most of your prospects — will close the tab before your "above the fold" image finishes painting. We have rebuilt sites where the only change required to lift conversion 30 percent was getting Largest Contentful Paint under 1.5 seconds.

If you do not know your p75 LCP on mobile, you do not know whether your homepage is working.

## 2. What does this company actually do?

The headline is the most expensive sentence on your site. Most startups waste it on a slogan. The hero of a high-converting startup site answers one question: what would I, the prospect, get if I worked with you?

"AI-powered platform for modern teams" answers nothing. "Turn sales calls into pipeline-ready follow-ups in 90 seconds" answers everything. The first sentence does not need to be poetic. It needs to be specific.

## 3. Is this for someone like me?

Inside the first viewport, the prospect is looking for a signal that this product is for them, not for a different segment. Logos help. A customer quote with a real name and a real role helps more. A vague "trusted by industry leaders" line helps less than nothing — it actively reads as bluffing.

If your customer base is real, name them. If it is not real yet, say "now in private beta with X teams" and be honest. Prospects forgive an early-stage startup. They do not forgive vagueness.

## 4. Can I trust the people behind this?

This check is invisible but constant. A prospect is looking for evidence that a competent team built this site, because the site is the only artifact of the team they can see. Broken links, mismatched font sizes, stock photos of people who do not work there, and a "we are excited to" announcement on the homepage all subtract trust.

Stock photos in particular are a tell. Anyone who has been in a sales call in the last five years can spot a stock photo at a glance, and the moment they do, the credibility deficit is real. We have a hard rule on every project: no stock photos. If a real photo does not exist yet, we ship a typographic hero and move on.

## 5. What do you want me to do next?

A homepage with seven calls to action has zero. A high-converting homepage has one primary call to action, repeated in the hero and at the bottom of the page, and one secondary call for prospects who are not ready yet.

The primary CTA is the one a customer would take if they were ready to buy. "Start a project". "Book a demo". "Get a quote". Not "learn more". Nobody clicks "learn more" on purpose.

## 6. Is this going to waste my time?

This is the silent check. Prospects are looking for any reason to leave, because leaving is free and reading is work. A homepage that looks busy, that auto-plays video with sound, that hijacks scroll, that throws a modal on entry — every one of these is a reason to leave.

The fastest way to fail this check is to forget that the prospect is doing you a favor by being on your site. Respect that. Cut everything that does not earn its place.

## What to do this week

If you have read this far and you are quietly auditing your own homepage in your head, here is the smallest useful thing you can do this week: open a real customer's session recording, watch the first 10 seconds, and write down everything that confused them. Then fix the top one.

Most homepages do not need a redesign. They need an editor.
