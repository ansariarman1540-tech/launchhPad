Most "launches" are not launches. They are soft openings to an audience the founder already knows, with metrics nobody is willing to look at, followed by six months of polish that nobody asks for.

A real launch puts a product in front of people who have never heard of you and asks them for money or a meaningful commitment. Thirty days is enough to do that, if you are honest about scope. Here is the playbook we run inside LaunchhPad, the same one we use on every Launch Sprint.

## Day 0 to 3: scope is the entire game

Founders lose more launches in the first three days than in the next twenty-seven. The cause is always the same: the scope is too big, and the team is too polite to say so out loud.

We open every sprint with a one-page brief that names exactly three things: the customer, the smallest version of the product that actually solves their problem, and the single number that proves it worked. If a feature is not required to move that number, it goes on a v2 list and we do not discuss it again until day thirty-one.

A useful test: if a junior PM read your scope doc cold, could they explain in one sentence why a customer would pay this week? If not, the scope is too vague, not too small.

## Day 4 to 21: build to a deployed environment, not a Figma file

The single biggest predictor of a successful launch is a preview environment running on real infrastructure by the end of week one. Not a Figma prototype. Not a localhost demo. A URL the founder can send to their mom.

Once that exists, the team's job becomes shipping into it every day. Small pull requests. Feature flags. Daily Loom updates. Weekly live demos with the founders and at least one real customer in the room.

This is also where most teams quietly add scope. Treat scope creep as a fire alarm, not a conversation. When something new comes up, the answer is "yes, after launch" or "yes, but we are cutting this other thing right now". There is no third option.

## Day 22 to 27: closed beta with twenty hand-picked users

A closed beta with twenty hand-picked users will teach you more than any survey, any focus group, and any internal review. Pick twenty people who match your target customer, watch them use the product live, and write down every dead-end they hit.

You are looking for two patterns: the moments where multiple users get stuck on the same step, and the moments where a user says something out loud that you wish you could put on the homepage. The first list becomes your week-four backlog. The second list becomes your launch copy.

## Day 28 to 30: launch is the start, not the finish

Public launch day is exciting and largely irrelevant. What matters is the thirty days after.

We launch with monitoring, error tracking, and an analytics dashboard wired to the success metric from day three. The first week post-launch is tuning, not handoff. We watch real sessions, fix the top three friction points, and ship a follow-up release inside fourteen days.

## The four mistakes that kill most launches

1. **Designing the v2 in week one.** If your team is debating a feature for the second cohort while the first cohort cannot sign up, the launch is already in trouble.
2. **Skipping the closed beta.** Public launch with no closed beta is a coin flip with worse odds. Twenty users in week four is non-negotiable.
3. **Treating launch day as a finish line.** The team that ships every day for thirty days post-launch wins the quarter. The team that disbands wins nothing.
4. **Hiding the success metric.** If you cannot tell the team on day twenty-nine what number you are trying to move, you are not running a launch — you are running a project.

A thirty-day launch is not a stunt. It is what happens when scope is honest, the team is senior, and the calendar is non-negotiable. The point is not speed for its own sake. The point is putting a real product in front of real customers before your team forgets why they started.
